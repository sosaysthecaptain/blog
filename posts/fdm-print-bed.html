The Print Bed
Feb 25, 2018

Adhesion

The first and most vexing problem to solve to make FDM work was that of the first layer. For an FDM print to succeed, it must stay well adhered throughout the duration of the print, but then come off the bed with a minimum of effort and force. Stratasys solved this problem with a two-part strategy of printing on a raft of soluble support material, which itself stuck forcefully (and permanently) to a replaceable ABS print bed. Although we wanted ultimately to add soluble support, it was important that our process not rely on it, and further important that our print surface be indefinitely reusable, so this solution was definitely out.

The internet had a great deal to say about the adhesion problem. A theme that we would quickly notice was that the large and lively open source community of the RepRap world had no shortage of suggestions, but there was no system for testing one against another and little interest in getting to the bottom of things. We realized that our role was largely to aggregate ideas and subject them to a level of rigorous side-by-side testing that no one else seemed willing to do, setting a high standard for acceptability.

We ran dozens of tests on our initial prototype printer, trying surfaces made out of different kinds of glass and plastic, heated to different temperatures, and treated with various substances. Some combinations we tried failed outright, but many worked well enough for prints to go to completion some of the time, but not reliably. After a lot of hours of printing, we figured out that PLA stuck reliably well to borosilicate glass at 50℃, if it was treated with AquaNet hairspray. We greatly appreciate the community’s help on this one—the hairspray we definitely wouldn’t have come up with on our own!

Bed levelness and rigidity

Given a suitable material and thermal situation, the bed still must be perfectly level relative to the plane of travel of the extruder. Moreover, the vertical offset between the two must be 100 ± 25 microns to achieve a good first layer. Getting the correct settings dialed in usually required about five false starts, and the force of popping a completed print off the bed was all it took to throw it all out of kilter, requiring the user to repeat the process for the next print.

Clearly, we needed a very rigid print bed with a robust leveling system. Our bed also served as the printer’s Y axis, so the bed had to be lightweight to avoid exacting a penalty allowable acceleration. We ended up with a double-decker design, in which an aluminum undercarriage was supported on two widely-spaced linear rails and connected to the borosilicate bed by four polycarbonate corner blocks, themselves glued to the glass. The corner blocks sat on neoprene washers, such that by tightening the screws that held them to the carriage, the washers could be variably compressed. It was thus possible to adjust the relative height of each corner individually.

The notion of gluing directly to the back of the borosilicate, ridiculous though this sounds, was novel at the time. The heaters commonly used in 2012 were made from (fiberglass) PCBs, and expanded at a different rate than glass. If they were glued to the glass, this differential expansion caused a disasterous warping effect. Consequently, the leading design practice was to eschew any kind of adhesive and hold the whole stack together with binder clips on the corners, an expedient that clearly minimized the repeatability of the entire arrangement and also cut into the available print area. We replaced the PCB heater with a custom high-wattage Kapton heater, sourced from Alibaba. Thin and flexible, and attached with a flexible adhesive, this solved the warping problem and made the glued foot design work.

We finished our bed design with an adjustable endstop calibration screw for the Z axis to control the absolute vertical offset. Leveling the bed was still an arduous process, but it could now be done once and hold indefinitely.

Dynamic bed leveling

We declared victory over the bed leveling situation and carried on, but as the hours of printing racked up, we realized we were still unsatisfied with first layers. Having a full one-foot-square print bed was an important design spec of the Isis One. Most printers on the market at the time had small print volumes, and in any case lacked the reliability to print more than one or two items at a time. It was important to us to be able to run very long prints of many parts at a time. No matter how assiduously we leveled the bed, first layer height was never reliably right at the margins. We needed to do better still.

The notion of dynamic bed leveling—measuring the actual offset of the bed at a number of points, and using the printer’s vertical axis to compensate in real time—was implemented by Stratasys, but had not yet made it to the consumer market. We decided to bite the bullet and implement this as well.

The software implementation was comparatively easy, but installing a reliable, low-cost, and lightweight actuator to lower a probe proved more difficult. We ended up with a design based on an RC servo and, after troubleshooting a bizarre issue by which electrical noise from the stepper wiring induced a signal in the servo’s signal line causing it to jitter manically, had a workable system. Now at last you could load up the entire volume with parts, hit print, and walk away with confidence that bed adhesion issues would not cause your print to fail.
